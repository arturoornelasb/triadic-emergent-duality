# First Principles of Metaphysics (Philosophy of Science)

## Overview
Metaphysics, in the context of philosophy of science, investigates the fundamental nature of reality as revealed by or presupposed by scientific theories: the nature of causation, laws of nature, natural kinds, identity, and part-whole relationships. These are not questions that science itself answers but that underlie and frame scientific inquiry. "First principles" here means the foundational questions and major competing positions about the deep structure of reality that science investigates.

## Prerequisites
- **Logic:** Modal logic (possibility, necessity), counterfactual reasoning
- **Epistemology:** Knowledge, justification, scientific method
- **Physics:** Classical mechanics, quantum mechanics, thermodynamics
- **Philosophy of Language:** Reference, meaning, possible worlds

## First Principles

### PRINCIPLE 1: Causation
- **Formal Statement:** Causation is the relation by which one event (the cause) brings about another (the effect). Major analyses: (1) Regularity theory (Hume, 1748): C causes E iff events of type C are regularly followed by events of type E. Causation is nothing but constant conjunction plus temporal priority and contiguity. (2) Counterfactual theory (Lewis, 1973): C causes E iff, had C not occurred, E would not have occurred (formalized via possible worlds: E depends counterfactually on C if in the nearest possible world where C does not occur, E does not occur). (3) Interventionist theory (Woodward, 2003): C causes E iff there exists an intervention on C that changes E. (4) Mechanistic account (Machamer, Darden, Craver, 2000): causal explanation involves identifying the mechanism linking C to E.
- **Plain Language:** What does it mean for one thing to cause another? Hume said it is just a pattern: one thing always follows another. Lewis said it means that if the cause had not happened, the effect would not have either. Woodward said causation is about what happens when you intervene and change something. Each view captures something important about how scientists think about causes.
- **Historical Context:** Hume (1748) argued that we never directly observe causation, only regular succession. Kant responded that causation is a necessary category of understanding. The 20th-century debate was transformed by David Lewis's counterfactual analysis (1973) and by the interventionist approach (Pearl, 2000; Woodward, 2003) which connects causation to experimental methodology and causal modeling.
- **Depends On:** Modal logic (counterfactuals, possible worlds), empiricism vs. rationalism
- **Implications:** Causation is central to scientific explanation: most scientific explanations are causal. The interventionist account connects directly to experimental design and causal inference in statistics (randomized controlled trials). Pearl's causal calculus (do-calculus) provides a formal framework for causal reasoning from observational data. The metaphysics of causation thus has direct implications for scientific methodology.

### PRINCIPLE 2: Laws of Nature
- **Formal Statement:** Laws of nature are the fundamental regularities or principles that govern the behavior of the natural world. Major accounts: (1) Regularity (Humean) view (Lewis, Mill-Ramsey-Lewis): Laws are the axioms of the best deductive system that balances simplicity and strength in summarizing the totality of particular facts. There is no metaphysical necessity behind laws; they are just the best summary of what happens. (2) Necessitarian view (Dretske, Tooley, Armstrong -- DTA account): Laws express necessary connections between universals. N(F, G) means that the universal F necessitates the universal G. Laws are not merely patterns but genuine constraints on reality. (3) Dispositional essentialism (Bird, 2007): Laws flow from the essential dispositional properties of natural kinds (e.g., electrons essentially have charge, and the laws of electromagnetism express this).
- **Plain Language:** Are the laws of physics necessary truths about the universe, or are they just descriptions of patterns that happen to hold? The Humean says laws are just efficient summaries of what happens; the necessitarian says laws genuinely constrain what can happen; the essentialist says laws flow from the natures of the things involved. This debate shapes how we understand the role of laws in explanation, prediction, and counterfactual reasoning.
- **Historical Context:** Hume (1748) originated the regularity view. The Best Systems Account was developed by Mill, Ramsey (1928), and Lewis (1973, 1994). The DTA account was independently proposed by Dretske (1977), Tooley (1977), and Armstrong (1983). Dispositional essentialism emerged from Shoemaker (1980) and was developed by Ellis (2001) and Bird (2007). The debate remains unresolved and central to philosophy of science.
- **Depends On:** Causation (Principle 1), modal logic (necessity, possibility)
- **Implications:** The nature of laws affects how we understand scientific explanation (are explanations tracking necessary connections or mere regularities?), counterfactual reasoning (can laws support counterfactuals?), and the relationship between fundamental and special sciences. The Humean view fits well with empiricism but struggles with counterfactuals; the necessitarian view supports counterfactuals but posits mysterious necessary connections.

### PRINCIPLE 3: Natural Kinds
- **Formal Statement:** Natural kinds are categories that "carve nature at its joints" (Plato, *Phaedrus*): they correspond to real, mind-independent divisions in nature. Examples: chemical elements (gold, oxygen), biological species, fundamental particles. Key views: (1) Essentialist realism (Kripke, 1980; Putnam, 1975): natural kinds have hidden essences (e.g., water is essentially H2O; gold is essentially the element with atomic number 79). Kind terms are rigid designators. (2) Homeostatic property cluster (Boyd, 1991): natural kinds are defined by clusters of co-occurring properties maintained by underlying causal mechanisms, without strict essences. (3) Eliminativism/nominalism: there are no natural kinds; all classification is conventional.
- **Plain Language:** Is "gold" a real category in nature, or just a label we invented? Most philosophers and scientists think some categories are genuinely natural -- gold really is a distinct kind of stuff, defined by its atomic structure. But not all categories used in science may be this tidy: "species" in biology and "mental disorder" in psychiatry are harder cases. The question of which categories are real and which are conventional is fundamental to science.
- **Historical Context:** Aristotle distinguished natural kinds from artifacts. Kripke (1980, *Naming and Necessity*) and Putnam (1975, "The Meaning of 'Meaning'") revived essentialism about natural kinds. Boyd (1991) proposed the homeostatic property cluster view to handle biological species. John Dupre (1993, *The Disorder of Things*) argued for a pluralist view: nature may not have a single correct set of joints.
- **Depends On:** Philosophy of language (reference, rigid designation), causation (Principle 1), laws of nature (Principle 2)
- **Implications:** Natural kinds underpin scientific taxonomy, induction (we project properties discovered in samples to the entire kind), and laws (laws apply to natural kinds). The question of whether biological species are natural kinds connects to the philosophy of biology (species problem). The debate affects the social sciences: are "depression," "race," or "intelligence" natural kinds?

### PRINCIPLE 4: Identity and Persistence
- **Formal Statement:** The problem of identity asks: what makes an object at one time the same object at a later time? Major views: (1) Endurantism: objects are wholly present at each moment of their existence; persistence is identity over time. Change is accounted for by time-indexed properties (being-red-at-t1, being-blue-at-t2). (2) Perdurantism (temporal parts theory): objects persist by having distinct temporal parts at each moment, just as they have spatial parts. An object is a four-dimensional "worm" through spacetime. (3) Criteria of identity vary by kind: for physical objects, spatiotemporal continuity and causal continuity; for persons, psychological continuity (Locke, Parfit) or biological continuity. Leibniz's Law: if x = y, then every property of x is a property of y (indiscernibility of identicals).
- **Plain Language:** Is the ship rebuilt plank by plank the same ship (Ship of Theseus)? Are you the same person you were 10 years ago, when every cell in your body has been replaced? The problem of identity asks what makes something the same thing over time. Some say objects endure through time as wholes; others say they are spread out through time like worms through spacetime, and what we call "the object at time t" is just a temporal slice.
- **Historical Context:** The Ship of Theseus paradox dates to ancient Greece. John Locke (1690) proposed that personal identity consists in continuity of consciousness. The modern endurantism vs. perdurantism debate was shaped by W.V.O. Quine (1960), David Lewis (1986), and Sider (2001). Relativity theory's spacetime framework lends support to perdurantism (objects as four-dimensional entities in spacetime).
- **Depends On:** Logic (Leibniz's Law), philosophy of time, philosophy of physics (spacetime)
- **Implications:** Central to personal identity (ethics, law, neuroscience of consciousness), philosophy of physics (quantum particles -- are they individuals?), and biology (what is an organism? what is a species over time?). The choice between endurantism and perdurantism has implications for how we understand change, constitution, and the relationship between objects and their parts.

### PRINCIPLE 5: Mereology (Part-Whole Relations)
- **Formal Statement:** Mereology is the theory of part-whole relations. Classical extensional mereology (CEM, Lesniewski 1916; Goodman and Leonard 1940) defines: (1) Parthood is reflexive, antisymmetric, and transitive. (2) Supplementation: if x is a proper part of y, then there exists a z that is part of y and does not overlap x. (3) Unrestricted composition (universalism): for any objects, there exists a mereological sum (fusion) that has exactly those objects as parts. Alternatives: (a) Restricted composition (van Inwagen, 1990): composition occurs only for certain arrangements (e.g., when parts constitute a life). (b) Nihilism: there are no composite objects, only simples. The Special Composition Question (van Inwagen): Under what conditions do parts compose a whole?
- **Plain Language:** When do parts make a whole? If you have a pile of bricks, is the pile an object? What about a house made of bricks? Classical mereology says any collection of things automatically forms a larger thing (a sum). But this seems to make strange objects: the sum of your left shoe and the Eiffel Tower. The question of when composition happens is one of the most debated in metaphysics.
- **Historical Context:** Mereology was formalized by Stanislaw Lesniewski (1916) and independently by Nelson Goodman and Henry Leonard (1940). Peter van Inwagen's *Material Beings* (1990) posed the Special Composition Question and revitalized the debate. David Lewis (1991) defended unrestricted composition. The debate connects to philosophy of physics (what are the fundamental simples?) and to philosophy of biology (what constitutes an organism?).
- **Depends On:** Logic (formal mereological axioms), identity (Principle 4)
- **Implications:** Mereology underpins ontology: what objects exist? If unrestricted composition is true, there are far more objects than common sense suggests. If nihilism is true, there are far fewer (only fundamental particles and fields, no tables or chairs). The debate connects to philosophy of physics (do quantum fields have parts?), philosophy of biology (are organisms sums of cells?), and cognitive science (is the mind a part of the body?).

### PRINCIPLE 6: Determinism and Indeterminism
- **Formal Statement:** Determinism holds that the state of the world at any time, together with the laws of nature, uniquely determines the state at all future times. Formally (Laplace's demon, 1814): given complete knowledge of the state at t0 and all laws, every state at t > t0 is fixed. Classical Newtonian mechanics is deterministic (with technical caveats: Norton's dome). Quantum mechanics (standard/Copenhagen interpretation) is indeterministic: the outcome of a measurement is genuinely random, governed only by probabilities (Born rule). Chaotic determinism: deterministic systems can exhibit sensitive dependence on initial conditions, making long-term prediction practically impossible even though the dynamics are fully determined.
- **Plain Language:** Is the future completely determined by the present and the laws of physics, or is there genuine randomness in the world? Classical physics said yes (deterministic). Quantum mechanics says no: at the fundamental level, outcomes are genuinely random. But even in a deterministic universe, chaos can make prediction practically impossible. The question of determinism has profound implications for free will, scientific prediction, and the nature of probability.
- **Historical Context:** Laplace (1814) articulated the deterministic ideal. The development of quantum mechanics (1920s-30s: Heisenberg, Born, Bohr) introduced fundamental indeterminism. The interpretive debate continues: the Many-Worlds interpretation (Everett, 1957) restores determinism at the level of the universal wave function. The discovery of chaos (Lorenz, 1963; Poincare, 1890s) showed that determinism does not imply predictability.
- **Depends On:** Laws of nature (Principle 2), causation (Principle 1), physics (QM, classical mechanics)
- **Implications:** Determinism/indeterminism shapes debates about free will (compatibilism, libertarianism, hard determinism), the nature of probability (objective vs. subjective), and the interpretation of quantum mechanics. In the special sciences, the question is whether higher-level laws (biology, economics) could be deterministic even if fundamental physics is not, and vice versa.

### PRINCIPLE 7: Ontological Categories (Substance, Property, Relation)

- **Formal Statement:** Ontological categories are the most general kinds of being. The traditional Aristotelian framework distinguishes: (1) Substance (ousia): the primary bearers of properties; that which exists independently (Aristotle: individual objects; Descartes: res cogitans and res extensa; Spinoza: only God/Nature is substance). (2) Property (quality, attribute): features or characteristics that substances possess (redness, mass, charge). Properties may be intrinsic (held independently of other objects) or extrinsic/relational. (3) Relation: a feature that holds between two or more entities (taller than, to the left of, gravitationally attracted to). Relations may be internal (determined by the intrinsic properties of the relata) or external (not so determined). The debate over ontological categories asks: which categories are fundamental? Are properties universals or tropes (particular instances of a property)? Are relations reducible to properties, or are they fundamental?
- **Plain Language:** What are the most basic kinds of things that exist? There are objects (like tables and electrons), properties of objects (like being red or having mass), and relations between objects (like being taller than). But which of these is really fundamental? Can you have properties without objects? Relations without relata? Philosophers disagree about what the basic furniture of the universe really is.
- **Historical Context:** Aristotle (*Categories*, ~350 BCE) provided the first systematic ontological category scheme. The substance-property distinction was central to medieval philosophy (Aquinas). The modern debate was revived by Russell (1903, *Principles of Mathematics*, on the reality of relations), Armstrong (1978, *Universals and Scientific Realism*), and Lowe (2006, *The Four-Category Ontology*). Process philosophy (Whitehead, 1929) challenges substance ontology by making events, not substances, fundamental.
- **Depends On:** Logic (predication, quantification), philosophy of language (reference)
- **Implications:** The choice of fundamental ontological categories shapes metaphysics and philosophy of science. If relations are fundamental (structural realism), then the structure of the world is more important than the intrinsic nature of its constituents. If tropes replace universals, then each instance of "redness" is a distinct particular. The ontological category scheme adopted has implications for the interpretation of quantum mechanics (are particles substances? or are only fields real?), the philosophy of space and time, and the foundations of mathematics.

### PRINCIPLE 8: Causation (Hume's Regularity, Counterfactual, Interventionist)

- **Formal Statement:** The analysis of causation has produced three major families of theories, each capturing different aspects of causal reasoning: (1) Hume's regularity theory: C causes E iff events of type C are constantly conjoined with events of type E, with C temporally prior to E. Causation is nothing more than observed regularity; there is no necessary connection (Hume, 1748). (2) Counterfactual theory (Lewis, 1973): C causes E iff, in the closest possible world where C does not occur, E does not occur. Causation is analyzed via counterfactual dependence in a framework of possible worlds. (3) Interventionist theory (Woodward, 2003; Pearl, 2000): C causes E iff there is a possible intervention on C that would change E, holding other variables fixed. Formalized using structural equation models and directed acyclic graphs (DAGs). Each theory faces distinctive challenges: regularity theory cannot distinguish genuine causes from mere correlations; counterfactual theory struggles with overdetermination and preemption; interventionism faces circularity concerns (defining intervention requires causal concepts).
- **Plain Language:** What does it mean for one thing to cause another? Hume said causation is just a pattern -- one thing always follows another, and we mistakenly infer a deeper connection. Lewis said a cause is something that, had it not happened, the effect would not have happened either. Woodward said a cause is something you can manipulate to change the effect. Each view captures part of the truth: regularity, dependence, and control are all aspects of causation. But no single theory satisfies everyone.
- **Historical Context:** The three theories represent the main threads of causation analysis since Hume (1748). Lewis (1973, *Counterfactuals*) formalized the counterfactual approach using possible worlds semantics. Woodward (2003, *Making Things Happen*) developed the interventionist approach, which connects directly to experimental methodology. Pearl (2000, *Causality*) provided the mathematical framework (do-calculus, structural causal models) that has been adopted in statistics, epidemiology, and AI.
- **Depends On:** Principle 1 (causation overview), modal logic, probability theory
- **Implications:** The choice of causal theory affects scientific methodology. The interventionist account connects directly to experimental design (randomized controlled trials intervene on causes). The counterfactual account underlies "what if" reasoning in science and policy. Pearl's causal calculus enables causal inference from observational data. In physics, the nature of causation connects to the arrow of time, the interpretation of quantum mechanics, and the question of whether fundamental physics is causal at all.

### PRINCIPLE 9: Personal Identity (Locke, Hume, Bundle Theory)

- **Formal Statement:** Personal identity asks: what makes a person at one time the same person at a later time? Major views: (1) Psychological continuity (Locke, 1690): personal identity consists in continuity of consciousness -- specifically, memory. You are the same person as your past self because you can (in principle) remember your past experiences. Parfit (1984) refined this: what matters is psychological continuity and connectedness (overlapping chains of memories, intentions, and character traits), and identity may not be what matters. (2) Bundle theory (Hume, 1739): the self is nothing but a bundle of perceptions in constant flux. There is no underlying substance (soul, ego) that persists; the self is a fiction we construct from associated mental events. (3) Biological/animalism (Olson, 1997): you are identical with a biological organism; personal identity is biological continuity. (4) Narrative identity (Ricoeur, 1992): personal identity is constituted by the stories we tell about ourselves.
- **Plain Language:** What makes you the same person today as you were ten years ago? Is it your memories? Your body? Your brain? An immaterial soul? Locke said it is memory -- you are the person whose experiences you can remember. Hume denied there is any persisting self at all; we are just bundles of experiences. These questions are not just academic: they matter for moral responsibility, legal punishment, and the ethics of brain transplants.
- **Historical Context:** Locke (1690, *Essay Concerning Human Understanding*) first proposed the memory theory. Hume (1739, *A Treatise of Human Nature*) denied the existence of a persisting self. Reid (1785) objected that Locke's theory leads to paradoxes (the brave officer). Derek Parfit (1984, *Reasons and Persons*) argued that personal identity is not what matters -- what matters is psychological continuity, which can hold in branching cases (fission) where strict identity fails.
- **Depends On:** Principle 4 (identity and persistence), philosophy of mind, ethics (moral responsibility)
- **Implications:** Personal identity connects metaphysics to ethics and law. If Parfit is right that identity is not what matters, this has profound implications for self-interest, moral responsibility, and the fear of death. In philosophy of mind, the debate connects to consciousness and the neural correlates of selfhood. In bioethics, it affects questions about advance directives, brain death, and personal survival under radical neural modification.

### PRINCIPLE 10: Free Will (Compatibilism, Libertarianism, Hard Determinism)

- **Formal Statement:** The free will problem asks whether free will is compatible with determinism and whether we in fact have free will. Major positions: (1) Hard determinism: the universe is deterministic (or sufficiently so); free will requires the ability to do otherwise; determinism precludes the ability to do otherwise; therefore, free will does not exist (Pereboom, 2001). (2) Libertarianism (metaphysical): free will exists and requires indeterminism at some level; agents are uncaused causes or agent-causes (Chisholm, 1964; Kane, 1996). (3) Compatibilism (Hume, 1748; Frankfurt, 1971; Dennett, 1984): free will is compatible with determinism. Free action is action that flows from the agent's own desires and reasoning, free from external compulsion. Frankfurt (1971): free will consists in having the will you want to have (second-order volitions align with first-order desires). (4) Hard incompatibilism (Pereboom): free will is incompatible with both determinism and indeterminism.
- **Plain Language:** Do you have free will? If the laws of physics determine everything, how can your choices be genuinely free? Hard determinists say they cannot -- free will is an illusion. Libertarians (in the philosophical sense) say that genuine freedom requires some break in the causal chain, perhaps at the quantum level. Compatibilists -- the majority of philosophers -- say that free will is not about escaping causation but about acting from your own desires without external coercion. You are free when you do what you want, even if your wanting is itself caused.
- **Historical Context:** The free will debate has ancient roots (Stoics, Epicurus). Hume (1748) and Mill (1859) defended compatibilism. Kant tried to reconcile freedom with determinism via the noumenal/phenomenal distinction. In the 20th century, Frankfurt (1971) revitalized compatibilism with his hierarchical model. Kane (1996) developed the most sophisticated libertarian theory. Libet's experiments (1983) on the timing of conscious will and recent neuroscience (Haynes et al., 2008) have added empirical dimensions to the debate.
- **Depends On:** Principle 6 (determinism), causation (Principle 1), philosophy of mind, ethics
- **Implications:** Free will is central to moral responsibility, legal punishment, and our self-understanding as agents. If hard determinism is correct, retributive punishment may be unjustified and the entire framework of moral praise and blame requires revision. Compatibilism preserves moral responsibility within a deterministic framework. The debate connects to neuroscience (do neural events determine actions before conscious awareness?), AI (can machines be morally responsible?), and political philosophy (justification of punishment).

### PRINCIPLE 11: Possible Worlds (Lewis, Kripke)

- **Formal Statement:** Possible worlds are ways things might have been. They provide the standard framework for analyzing modal concepts (necessity, possibility, contingency). Key positions: (1) Modal realism (David Lewis, 1986): possible worlds are concrete, spatiotemporally disconnected universes that are just as real as the actual world. The actual world is simply the world we happen to inhabit. There is no metaphysically privileged world. (2) Ersatzism (abstractionism): possible worlds are abstract entities -- maximally consistent sets of propositions (Plantinga), recombinations of elements from the actual world (Armstrong), or linguistic descriptions. They are useful fictions or abstract representations, not concrete realities. (3) Kripke (1980): possible worlds are "stipulated, not discovered." They are counterfactual scenarios we describe, not distant planets we peer at through a telescope. Kripke used possible worlds to argue for the necessity of identity and the existence of necessary a posteriori truths (e.g., "water is H2O" is necessarily true but known empirically).
- **Plain Language:** Could things have been different? Could you have been born in a different country? Could water have been something other than H2O? "Possible worlds" is the philosophical tool for making sense of these questions. Lewis took possible worlds literally -- every way things could be is a real, concrete universe somewhere. Most philosophers think possible worlds are useful abstractions, not real places. Either way, they are indispensable for analyzing necessity, possibility, and counterfactual reasoning.
- **Historical Context:** Leibniz (1710) introduced the idea that God chose the best of all possible worlds. Kripke (1959, 1980) developed possible worlds semantics for modal logic and used it to argue for rigid designation and necessary a posteriori truths. David Lewis (1986, *On the Plurality of Worlds*) defended modal realism. Possible worlds semantics is now standard in logic, philosophy of language, and metaphysics.
- **Depends On:** Modal logic (necessity, possibility), philosophy of language (reference, rigid designation)
- **Implications:** Possible worlds are the standard tool for analyzing counterfactual reasoning (central to causation, Principle 1), laws of nature (Principle 2), and scientific explanation. Kripke's arguments about necessary a posteriori truths (like "water is H2O") have implications for the philosophy of science: scientific discoveries can reveal necessary truths about the nature of things. Lewis's modal realism, while controversial, provides the most straightforward semantics for counterfactuals and has been deeply influential in metaphysics and philosophy of science.

### PRINCIPLE 12: Universals vs Particulars (Plato, Aristotle, Nominalism)

- **Formal Statement:** The problem of universals asks: do properties (redness, triangularity, charge) exist independently of the particular objects that have them? Major positions: (1) Platonic realism (transcendent universals): universals exist in a realm separate from particular objects. Redness itself exists even if all red things are destroyed. (2) Aristotelian realism (immanent universals): universals exist but only in the particular objects that instantiate them. Redness exists only in red things. (3) Nominalism: only particular objects exist; there are no universals. Resemblance nominalism (Price, 1953): things are "red" because they resemble each other. Predicate nominalism: "red" is just a word we apply to similar things. Trope theory (Williams, 1953; Campbell, 1990): properties are particular instances (tropes), not universals. Each red thing has its own redness-trope, numerically distinct from every other.
- **Plain Language:** When we say two different roses are both red, is there one thing -- redness -- that they both share? Plato said yes: redness is a Form existing in a separate realm. Aristotle said redness exists only in the roses themselves. Nominalists say there is no such thing as redness itself; we just use the word "red" for things that look similar. This ancient debate has real consequences for science: do natural properties (mass, charge, spin) exist independently, or are they just useful labels?
- **Historical Context:** Plato (*Republic*, *Phaedo*, ~380 BCE) introduced the theory of Forms. Aristotle rejected transcendent Forms but accepted immanent universals. Medieval nominalists (Ockham, ~1320) denied universals. The modern debate was revitalized by Russell (1912), Armstrong (1978, *Universals and Scientific Realism*), and Lewis (1986). Trope theory was developed by Williams (1953) and Campbell (1990) as a nominalist alternative that avoids some objections to traditional nominalism.
- **Depends On:** Logic (predication), philosophy of language, metaphysics (ontological categories, Principle 7)
- **Implications:** The universals debate affects the interpretation of scientific laws (do laws govern universals or just describe patterns among particulars?), mathematics (are mathematical objects universals?), and natural kinds (Principle 3). Armstrong's necessitarian account of laws (Principle 2) requires universals. Lewis's Humean account does not. In philosophy of physics, the debate connects to the ontological status of properties like charge and spin: are they real universals, or just useful theoretical posits?

### PRINCIPLE 13: Supervenience

- **Formal Statement:** A set of properties A supervenes on a set of properties B iff there can be no difference in A-properties without a difference in B-properties. Formally, for all possible worlds w1 and w2, if w1 and w2 are B-indiscernible (agree on all B-properties), then they are A-indiscernible (agree on all A-properties). Types: (1) Weak supervenience: within any possible world, B-indiscernible entities are A-indiscernible. (2) Strong supervenience (Kim, 1984): across all possible worlds, B-indiscernible entities are A-indiscernible. (3) Global supervenience: worlds that are B-indiscernible are A-indiscernible. Mental properties supervene on physical properties: no mental change without a physical change (widely accepted, even by non-reductive physicalists). Moral properties supervene on natural properties: no moral difference without a natural difference. Supervenience is weaker than reduction: A supervening on B does not entail that A-properties are identical to or definable in terms of B-properties.
- **Plain Language:** Supervenience captures the idea of dependence without identity. The mental depends on the physical: if you duplicate every physical fact about my brain, you duplicate my mental states. But this does not mean that mental states are identical to brain states -- just that they cannot vary independently. Supervenience is a minimal commitment: almost everyone in philosophy of mind accepts it, whether they are reductionists or not. It says the lower level constrains the upper level without specifying the exact nature of the relationship.
- **Historical Context:** The concept was used informally by G. E. Moore (1922) and R. M. Hare (1952) in ethics. Jaegwon Kim (1984, 1993) developed the concept rigorously and made it central to the mind-body debate. Davidson (1970) used supervenience in his anomalous monism: mental events supervene on physical events, but there are no strict psychophysical laws. The concept has been applied across philosophy: aesthetics on physical properties, moral on natural, semantic on syntactic, biological on physical-chemical.
- **Depends On:** Modal logic (possible worlds), metaphysics (properties, identity), Principle 7 (ontological categories)
- **Implications:** Supervenience is the minimal bridge between levels of description. It is widely used in philosophy of mind to state the physicalist thesis without committing to reductionism. However, Kim's exclusion argument (Principle 7 in Philosophy of Mind) uses supervenience to argue that non-reductive physicalism is unstable: if mental properties supervene on physical properties and the physical is causally closed, mental causation is threatened. Supervenience has also been applied in the philosophy of biology (biological on physical-chemical), philosophy of science (macro on micro), and ethics.

### PRINCIPLE 14: Trope Theory

- **Formal Statement:** Trope theory (Williams, 1953; Campbell, 1990; Maurin, 2002) holds that the fundamental ontological category is the trope: a particular, non-repeatable property instance. The redness of this particular rose is a trope, numerically distinct from the redness of any other rose. Tropes replace both universals and bare particulars: (1) Ordinary objects are bundles of compresent tropes (tropes that co-occur in the same location). (2) Resemblance between objects is explained by similarity among tropes, not by shared universals. (3) Tropes are abstract particulars: they are particular (non-repeatable) yet abstract (they are properties, not concrete objects). Natural laws, on the trope view, describe regularities among tropes or are grounded in the dispositional nature of tropes.
- **Plain Language:** Instead of saying that two roses share the universal "redness," trope theory says each rose has its own particular redness -- a unique instance that resembles but is not identical to the other. Objects are just bundles of these particular property instances. This avoids the mysterious realm of universals (Plato's Forms) while still taking properties seriously. Each red thing has its own redness, each massive thing has its own mass-trope, and similarity between things is explained by resemblance among their tropes.
- **Historical Context:** D. C. Williams (1953), "On the Elements of Being," introduced the term "trope." Keith Campbell (1990, *Abstract Particulars*) developed trope theory as a full metaphysical system. Anna-Sofia Maurin (2002) provided further refinements. Trope theory is an alternative to both Platonic realism (transcendent universals), Aristotelian realism (immanent universals), and traditional nominalism (no properties at all).
- **Depends On:** Principle 12 (universals vs. particulars), metaphysics (ontological categories, Principle 7), logic
- **Implications:** Trope theory offers a parsimonious ontology: one category (tropes) does the work of two (universals and particulars). It avoids the epistemological problem of Platonism (how do we access abstract universals?) because tropes are located where the object is. Challenges include the companionship problem (explaining why certain tropes always co-occur), the imperfect community problem (trope resemblance classes may not carve out natural properties cleanly), and questions about identity conditions for tropes.

### PRINCIPLE 15: Modal Realism (Lewis)

- **Formal Statement:** Modal realism (David Lewis, 1986, *On the Plurality of Worlds*) holds that possible worlds are concrete, spatiotemporally disconnected universes, each as real as the actual world. Key theses: (1) There exist infinitely many possible worlds, one for every way things could be. (2) The actual world is not metaphysically privileged; "actual" is an indexical, like "here" -- it refers to the world the speaker happens to inhabit. (3) Possible worlds are spatiotemporally isolated from each other: no causal or spatiotemporal relations hold between distinct worlds. (4) Every way a world could be is a way some world is (plenitude). (5) Individuals are world-bound: they exist in exactly one world. Cross-world identity is replaced by counterpart relations: in another world, a counterpart of you (an entity sufficiently similar to you) may exist, but it is not numerically identical to you.
- **Plain Language:** David Lewis took possible worlds literally. When we say "things could have been different," Lewis says there really is a concrete universe where they are different -- it is just causally disconnected from ours. There is a universe where you are a marine biologist, and another where dinosaurs never went extinct. These universes are just as real as ours; we just cannot interact with them. Most philosophers find this extravagant, but Lewis argued it provides the best explanation of modality, counterfactuals, and a host of other philosophical concepts.
- **Historical Context:** Lewis (1986) developed modal realism as the most systematic metaphysics of modality. The view builds on Lewis's earlier work on counterfactuals (1973), which used possible worlds to analyze "if...then" statements. Modal realism is widely discussed but has few defenders, as most philosophers prefer ersatz alternatives (possible worlds as abstract objects, sets of propositions, or linguistic constructions). However, Lewis's arguments for modal realism remain some of the most powerful in metaphysics.
- **Depends On:** Principle 11 (possible worlds), modal logic, philosophy of language (indexicals, reference)
- **Implications:** Modal realism provides the most straightforward semantics for counterfactuals, which are central to causation (Principle 1) and scientific explanation. It yields a simple account of properties (as sets of possible individuals), propositions (as sets of possible worlds), and supervenience. The main objection is the "incredulous stare" (Lewis's own term): the sheer ontological extravagance of positing infinitely many concrete universes. Epistemological concerns also arise: how could we know about these other worlds if they are causally disconnected from us?

### PRINCIPLE 16: Grounding

- **Formal Statement:** Grounding is a metaphysical relation that holds between facts, where one set of facts (the grounds) metaphysically explains or gives rise to another fact (the grounded). Formally, if [p] grounds [q], then the fact that q obtains in virtue of the fact that p obtaining. Key features: (1) Asymmetry: if [p] grounds [q], then [q] does not ground [p]. (2) Transitivity: if [p] grounds [q] and [q] grounds [r], then [p] grounds [r]. (3) Irreflexivity: no fact grounds itself. (4) Non-causal: grounding is a constitutive or structural relation, not a causal one. (5) Necessitation: if [p] grounds [q], then necessarily, if [p] then [q]. Examples: mental facts are grounded in physical facts; the fact that the mereological sum exists is grounded in the existence of the parts; the fact that an action is wrong is grounded in the harm it causes.
- **Plain Language:** Grounding is the "in virtue of" relation. Why is this vase fragile? In virtue of the molecular structure of the glass. Why is it wrong to lie? In virtue of the harm deception causes. Grounding captures the intuition that some facts are more fundamental than others, and that less fundamental facts hold because of more fundamental ones. It is not causation (the molecular structure does not cause the fragility in a temporal sense) but a deeper constitutive explanation.
- **Historical Context:** The concept of grounding was developed in the 2000s-2010s by Kit Fine (2001, 2012), Gideon Rosen (2010), Jonathan Schaffer (2009), and Fabrice Correia (2005). It rapidly became one of the central topics in analytic metaphysics. Schaffer's "priority monism" uses grounding as the structuring relation of reality. Critics (Wilson, 2014; Koslicki, 2015) argue that grounding is obscure, not unified, or reducible to other notions (necessitation, supervenience).
- **Depends On:** Metaphysics (ontological categories, Principle 7), modal logic, explanation theory
- **Implications:** Grounding provides a framework for stating physicalism (all facts are grounded in physical facts), moral naturalism (moral facts are grounded in natural facts), and mathematical structuralism (mathematical facts are grounded in structural facts). It offers a more fine-grained tool than supervenience: supervenience says there is a necessary connection between levels, but grounding says which level is explanatorily prior. In philosophy of science, grounding captures the intuition that higher-level sciences describe facts that hold in virtue of lower-level facts, without requiring reduction.

### PRINCIPLE 17: Temporal Parts vs. Endurantism

- **Formal Statement:** The persistence debate asks how objects persist through time. Two main views: (1) Endurantism (three-dimensionalism): objects are wholly present at each moment of their existence. The object at t1 is numerically identical to the object at t2 -- it endures through time. Change is accounted for by time-indexed properties (being-red-at-t1, being-blue-at-t2) or by relativizing predication to times. (2) Perdurantism (four-dimensionalism, temporal parts theory): objects persist by having distinct temporal parts at each moment, just as they have spatial parts at each location. An object is a four-dimensional "worm" through spacetime. What we call "the cat at noon" is a temporal part of the cat's four-dimensional extent. (3) Stage theory (exdurantism, Sider, 2001; Hawley, 2001): the fundamental entities are momentary stages, and persistence is a relation of "genidentity" or temporal counterpart between stages.
- **Plain Language:** When you say "that is the same cat I saw yesterday," what does "the same" mean? Endurantists say the cat is wholly present now, just as it was wholly present yesterday -- it is one three-dimensional thing that endures through time. Perdurantists say the cat is a four-dimensional object stretched through time, and what you see now is just the current temporal slice of a longer spacetime worm. Stage theorists say there is no worm -- there are just momentary stages, and what we call "persistence" is a relation between these stages.
- **Historical Context:** Endurantism is the common-sense view with roots in Aristotle. Perdurantism was developed by Quine (1960), Lewis (1986), and Hales and Johnson (2003). Sider (2001, *Four-Dimensionalism*) provided the most thorough defense of temporal parts. The debate connects to the philosophy of physics: special relativity's spacetime framework is often cited as supporting perdurantism, since it treats time as analogous to space. However, Balashov (2010) and others have argued that the physics does not decisively favor either view.
- **Depends On:** Principle 4 (identity and persistence), philosophy of time, philosophy of physics (spacetime)
- **Implications:** The persistence debate affects our understanding of change, constitution, coincidence (can two objects occupy the same space at the same time?), and vagueness (is the boundary of a persisting object vague in the temporal dimension?). In philosophy of physics, the debate connects to the interpretation of spacetime in relativity theory. In personal identity, the choice between endurantism and perdurantism shapes how we think about the persistence of persons and the ethics of future selves.

### PRINCIPLE 18: Truthmaker Theory
- **Formal Statement:** Truthmaker theory holds that for every truth, there is something in the world -- a truthmaker -- that makes it true. The truthmaker principle: if p is true, then there exists some entity E such that E necessitates the truth of p (E is the truthmaker for p). Formally, E makes p true iff (i) E exists, and (ii) necessarily, if E exists, then p is true. Key debates: (1) Truthmaker maximalism: every truth has a truthmaker (Armstrong, 2004). This is challenged by negative truths ("there are no unicorns" -- what makes this true?). (2) Truthmaker necessitarianism: the truthmaker must necessitate the truth, not merely be relevant to it. (3) Kinds of truthmakers: states of affairs (Armstrong), tropes (Mulligan, Simons, Smith, 1984), or entities of various categories. The theory provides a constraint on ontology: what you believe exists should be determined by what truthmakers the truths require.
- **Plain Language:** If it is true that "the cat is on the mat," something in the world must make it true. That something -- the state of affairs of the cat being on the mat -- is the truthmaker. Truthmaker theory asks: for every truth, what bit of reality is responsible for it? This turns out to be deeply challenging for negative truths (what makes it true that there are no dragons?) and for abstract truths (what makes it true that 2 + 2 = 4?). The theory forces us to be explicit about the ontological commitments of our beliefs.
- **Historical Context:** The truthmaker idea was developed by Kevin Mulligan, Peter Simons, and Barry Smith (1984) and D. M. Armstrong (1997, 2004, *Truth and Truthmakers*). It has roots in Russell's logical atomism (1918) and Wittgenstein's *Tractatus* (1921). The idea has become central to contemporary analytic metaphysics, generating a large literature on what truthmakers are, whether all truths need them, and how they connect to ontology.
- **Depends On:** Ontological categories (Principle 7), logic (truth, predication), possible worlds (Principle 11)
- **Implications:** Truthmaker theory provides a principled constraint on ontology: postulate only the entities needed to make the truths true. It connects truth to reality in a rigorous way and informs debates about realism, the ontological status of negative facts, and the relationship between language and the world. In philosophy of science, it asks: what in the world makes scientific theories true? The answer shapes our understanding of scientific realism and the ontological commitments of science.

### PRINCIPLE 19: Dispositions and Categorical Properties
- **Formal Statement:** The disposition/categorical distinction concerns the nature of properties. (1) Dispositional properties (powers) are defined by what their bearers would do in certain circumstances: fragility is the disposition to break when struck; charge is the disposition to produce and respond to electromagnetic fields. Dispositional properties are essentially modal: they involve counterfactuals about what would happen. (2) Categorical properties are non-modal "qualities" of objects (shape, structure) that ground dispositions but are not themselves defined in terms of what would happen. The debate: (a) Categoricalism (Armstrong, Lewis): all fundamental properties are categorical; dispositions are derivative. (b) Dispositionalism (Mumford, 1998; Bird, 2007): all or some fundamental properties are dispositional. Charge just is the power to attract/repel; there is nothing "behind" the disposition. (c) Neutral monism / powerful qualities (Martin, 1997; Heil, 2003): properties have both a categorical and a dispositional aspect -- they are two sides of the same coin.
- **Plain Language:** Is glass fragile because of some underlying structural property (categorical), or is fragility itself the fundamental nature of the glass? Categoricalists say the microstructure is what is real; fragility is just a shorthand for what the structure would do. Dispositionalists say the power (fragility, charge, mass) is what is real -- properties are fundamentally about what things can do, not about some inert quality "behind" the doing. This debate determines whether the laws of physics govern inert stuff from the outside or whether the laws flow from the active natures of things.
- **Historical Context:** The distinction traces to Locke (1690) on primary and secondary qualities. C. B. Martin (1994, 1997) and Mumford (1998, *Dispositions*) developed the modern dispositional account. Bird (2007, *Nature's Metaphysics*) argued that dispositionalism supports dispositional essentialism about laws: laws are necessary because they flow from the essential dispositions of natural kinds. Lewis (1997) defended the categorical view, arguing that properties are "quiddities" -- they have a nature independent of their causal role.
- **Depends On:** Laws of nature (Principle 2), natural kinds (Principle 3), ontological categories (Principle 7)
- **Implications:** If dispositionalism is correct, laws of nature are metaphysically necessary (they flow from the natures of things), overturning the Humean contingency of laws. If categoricalism is correct, the laws are contingent and the same property could play different causal roles in different possible worlds. The debate affects the interpretation of fundamental physics (is charge a disposition or a categorical property?), the philosophy of biology (are biological functions dispositional?), and the philosophy of mind (are mental states dispositions to behave?).

### PRINCIPLE 20: Structural Realism (Metaphysical)
- **Formal Statement:** Structural realism holds that what is real in the world is the structure described by our best scientific theories, rather than the intrinsic natures of the entities those theories posit. Two forms: (1) Epistemic structural realism (Worrall, 1989): we can know the structure of the world (the relations described by scientific equations) but not the intrinsic natures of the entities. The mathematical structure of theories is preserved through scientific revolutions even when the ontology changes (Fresnel's ether gave way to Maxwell's electromagnetic field, but the equations survived). (2) Ontic structural realism (Ladyman, 1998; French and Ladyman, 2003): structure is all there is. There are no individuals with intrinsic properties "underlying" the structure; the fundamental level of reality is purely structural (relational). Objects are nodes in a structure, not self-subsistent entities. Quantum mechanics supports this: quantum particles lack individuality (permutation invariance, the Leibniz identity problem for bosons).
- **Plain Language:** Structural realism says that what science really discovers is the structure of the world -- the mathematical relationships between things -- not the intrinsic nature of the things themselves. When science changes from one theory to another (from Newton to Einstein), the mathematical structure often survives even though the "stuff" changes. The radical version says there is no "stuff" at all: reality is purely structural, like a network of relations with no nodes underneath. Quantum physics, where particles lack clear individuality, seems to support this.
- **Historical Context:** John Worrall (1989), "Structural Realism: The Best of Both Worlds?" proposed epistemic structural realism as a compromise between scientific realism (theories describe the world truly) and anti-realism (theories change too often to be trusted). James Ladyman (1998) radicalized this into ontic structural realism, drawing on structuralism in mathematics and the identity problems of quantum mechanics. French and Ladyman (2003) developed the framework further. The position is now one of the leading alternatives in the scientific realism debate.
- **Depends On:** Natural kinds (Principle 3), laws of nature (Principle 2), philosophy of physics (quantum mechanics), universals (Principle 12)
- **Implications:** Structural realism provides a response to the pessimistic meta-induction (past theories were false, so current ones probably are too) by arguing that the structure of successful theories is preserved. Ontic structural realism has radical implications: if individuals are not fundamental, then the ontological categories of substance and property (Principle 7) must be revised. The position connects to debates about the interpretation of quantum mechanics, the nature of spacetime in general relativity, and the foundations of mathematics (mathematical structuralism).

### PRINCIPLE 21: Abstract Objects (Ontology of Abstracta)
- **Formal Statement:** The ontology of abstract objects asks whether entities that are non-spatiotemporal, non-causal, and mind-independent -- numbers, propositions, universals, sets, possible worlds -- genuinely exist, and if so, what their nature is. Major positions: (1) Platonism (Frege, Godel): abstract objects exist in a mind-independent realm. (2) Nominalism (Goodman, Quine): only concrete particulars exist; apparent reference to abstracta can be paraphrased away or treated as fiction. (3) Fictionalism (Field, 1980): abstract-object talk is useful fiction; mathematical statements are literally false but instrumentally valuable. (4) Neo-Fregean abstractionism (Wright, Hale): abstract objects are introduced by abstraction principles (e.g., Hume's Principle: the number of Fs = the number of Gs iff F and G are equinumerous). (5) Deflationism: the existence of abstract objects is a trivial consequence of our best theories and requires no deep metaphysical commitment.
- **Plain Language:** Do numbers, sets, and propositions really exist? They are not physical things you can touch or locate in space. Yet science, logic, and everyday reasoning seem to require them. The debate over abstract objects is one of the oldest in philosophy and remains unresolved: are they real but non-physical, or are they useful fictions, or something else entirely?
- **Historical Context:** Plato posited the Forms as the paradigm of abstracta. The modern debate was framed by Frege (1884), Quine (1948, "On What There Is"), Goodman and Quine (1947, nominalism), and Field (1980, fictionalism). Wright (1983) and Hale (2001) developed neo-Fregeanism. The debate connects to the indispensability argument (philosophy of mathematics) and to the question of what scientific theories commit us to ontologically.
- **Depends On:** Ontological categories (Principle 7), laws of nature (Principle 2), philosophy of mathematics
- **Implications:** The status of abstract objects affects every branch of philosophy: mathematics (are numbers real?), logic (do propositions exist?), ethics (are moral properties abstract?), and science (do laws of nature involve universals?). The debate shapes the boundary between empiricism and rationalism and determines how broadly we construe reality.

### PRINCIPLE 22: Modality and Essence
- **Formal Statement:** Modality concerns what is possible, necessary, and contingent. Essence concerns what properties are essential (constitutive of what a thing is) versus accidental (things a thing could lack while remaining itself). Key frameworks: (1) Possible worlds (Kripke, 1980; Lewis, 1986): a proposition is necessarily true iff it is true in all possible worlds; possibly true iff true in some world. A property is essential to an object iff the object has it in every world in which it exists. (2) Fine's critique (1994): essence is prior to modality, not definable in terms of it. Socrates is essentially human, but "existing iff 2+2=4" is necessarily true of Socrates without being essential to him. Essence is about the nature of the thing, not mere necessity. (3) Dispositional essentialism (Bird, 2007): the essential properties of natural kinds include their causal powers/dispositions; laws of nature flow from essences.
- **Plain Language:** Could things have been different? Could water have been something other than H2O? Could you have been someone else? Modality is about what is possible and what is necessary. Essence is about what makes a thing the thing it is -- what it could not lose without ceasing to be itself. Kit Fine argued that essence is more fundamental than possibility: knowing what a thing is tells you what is necessary about it, not the other way around.
- **Historical Context:** Aristotle distinguished essential from accidental properties. Kripke (1980, *Naming and Necessity*) revived essentialism, arguing that water is necessarily H2O and that Hesperus is necessarily Phosphorus. Lewis (1986, *On the Plurality of Worlds*) developed modal realism. Fine (1994, "Essence and Modality") argued that modality is grounded in essence, not vice versa, shifting the focus of metaphysical inquiry.
- **Depends On:** Possible worlds (Principle 11), natural kinds (Principle 3), laws of nature (Principle 2)
- **Implications:** Modality and essence are foundational to metaphysics: they underpin debates about laws, natural kinds, personal identity, and free will. Fine's essentialist turn has influenced metaphysics broadly, redirecting attention from modal logic to the question of what things fundamentally are. The debate connects to philosophy of science (are laws essential to the kinds they govern?) and to philosophy of mind (is consciousness essential to persons?).

### PRINCIPLE 23: Best Systems Account of Laws (Lewis-Ramsey)
- **Formal Statement:** The Best Systems Account (BSA; Mill, Ramsey, Lewis) defines laws of nature as the axioms of the deductive system that achieves the best balance of simplicity and strength (informational content) in summarizing all particular facts (the Humean mosaic). Formally: given the totality of particular matters of fact (the distribution of fundamental properties across spacetime), the laws are the theorems of the axiomatic system that best combines (1) simplicity (few axioms, simple mathematical form) and (2) strength (entailing as many truths as possible about the mosaic). There is no metaphysical necessity behind laws; they supervene on the totality of occurrent facts. Lewis (1994) added the requirement that the system use only natural predicates (avoiding gerrymandered predicates that trivially increase strength). Challenges: (1) the standards for simplicity and strength may be vague or language-dependent; (2) the BSA seems to allow laws that hold by cosmic accident; (3) it is unclear whether the BSA can account for the role of laws in supporting counterfactuals and grounding induction.
- **Plain Language:** What if the laws of physics are just the most efficient summary of everything that happens? There is no hidden force making things obey laws; the laws are simply the best compression of all the facts. Think of it like finding the shortest computer program that reproduces a dataset. This is the Humean view: the world is just a mosaic of events, and laws are patterns in that mosaic, not constraints on it.
- **Historical Context:** The BSA has roots in Mill (1843), was formalized by Ramsey (1928), and was developed in detail by Lewis (1973, 1994). The account has been defended and refined by Loewer (2007), Cohen and Callender (2009, the "Better Best Systems Account" extending to special sciences), and Dorst (2019). The BSA is the dominant Humean approach and is contrasted with the DTA (Dretske-Tooley-Armstrong) necessitarian account (Principle 2) and dispositional essentialism (Bird, 2007).
- **Depends On:** Laws of nature (Principle 2), supervenience (Principle 13), possible worlds (Principle 11)
- **Implications:** The BSA is the most influential Humean account of laws and has been extended to special-science laws (biology, economics). It raises deep questions about the relationship between laws, explanation, and counterfactuals. If laws are just summaries, can they explain anything? Can they support predictions about unobserved cases? The debate between the BSA and necessitarian accounts is one of the central issues in contemporary metaphysics of science.

### PRINCIPLE P24 — Ontological Dependence

**Formal Statement:**
Ontological dependence is the relation by which one entity depends for its existence or identity on another. Key varieties: (1) Existential dependence (rigid): x depends on y iff necessarily, if x exists then y exists. (2) Identity dependence: the identity of x depends on its relation to y (e.g., the identity of a set depends on its members). (3) Generic dependence: x depends on entities of kind F, but not on any particular entity. (4) Essential dependence (Fine, 1995): x depends on y iff y is part of the essence of x. Fine argues that essential dependence is more fundamental than modal (existential) dependence, since Socrates's essence includes being human but the singleton {Socrates} does not belong to Socrates's essence, despite the necessary co-existence of Socrates and {Socrates}. The relation connects to grounding (Principle 16): grounding is often mediated by ontological dependence, but the two notions are distinct (grounding relates facts; ontological dependence relates entities).

**Plain Language:**
Some things cannot exist without other things. A smile cannot exist without a face. A hole cannot exist without the material around it. A set cannot exist without its members. But there are subtle distinctions: does a thing depend on specific others, or just on some things of a certain kind? And is the dependence about existence or about what the thing is? These questions matter because they structure how we understand reality's hierarchical organization -- which things are fundamental and which are derivative.

**Historical Context:**
Aristotle distinguished between substances (self-standing) and accidents (dependent). Husserl (1901) analyzed part-whole dependence in the third *Logical Investigation*. Kit Fine (1995) developed the contemporary account using essence. Lowe (1998, 2006) provided a systematic taxonomy of ontological dependence relations. The concept is central to debates about fundamentality, grounding, and the structure of reality.

**Depends On:**
- Grounding (Principle 16)
- Ontological categories (Principle 7)
- Modality and essence (Principle 22)

**Implications:**
- Structures the debate about what is fundamental: fundamental entities are ontologically independent; derivative entities depend on them
- Essential for the philosophy of social kinds (institutions depend on collective attitudes), artifacts (depend on intentions), and abstract objects (do they depend on minds?)
- Connects metaphysics to the philosophy of mind (does the mental depend on the physical?) and the philosophy of mathematics (do numbers depend on structures?)

### PRINCIPLE P25 — Metaphysical Indeterminacy

**Formal Statement:**
Metaphysical indeterminacy is the thesis that the world itself, and not merely our descriptions of it, can be indeterminate. Classical vagueness theory locates indeterminacy in language (semantic vagueness) or knowledge (epistemic vagueness). But some philosophers argue for genuine ontic vagueness: worldly indeterminacy. Key proposals: (1) Barnes and Williams (2011): a precisificational framework where there are multiple admissible "sharpenings" of reality itself (not just of our descriptions), and no fact of the matter about which sharpening obtains. (2) Quantum mechanical indeterminacy (Calosi and Wilson, 2019): on some interpretations of QM, a quantum system can genuinely lack a determinate value for a property (spin-z = up or down) -- this is not our ignorance but a feature of reality. (3) Wilson (2013): the determinable-based account -- metaphysical indeterminacy occurs when an object has a determinable property (e.g., color) without having any specific determinate of it (e.g., a specific shade). The key philosophical question: is genuine worldly indeterminacy coherent, or does it inevitably collapse into epistemic or semantic vagueness?

**Plain Language:**
Is the world itself ever genuinely vague or fuzzy, or is vagueness always in our language and knowledge? When we say "the border between France and Switzerland is vague," is that because our description is imprecise or because the border is genuinely indeterminate? Quantum mechanics makes the question urgent: before measurement, does a particle really lack a definite position, or do we just not know it? Some philosophers argue that reality itself can be indeterminate -- and this is a feature, not a bug, of our best physical theories.

**Historical Context:**
Evans (1978) famously argued against ontic vagueness (the Evans argument). Barnes and Williams (2011) defended it via precisificational metaphysics. Wilson (2013) proposed the determinable-based account. Calosi and Wilson (2019) applied it to quantum mechanics. The topic connects metaphysics to philosophy of physics, philosophy of language, and logic.

**Depends On:**
- Determinism (Principle 6)
- Identity and persistence (Principle 4)
- Possible worlds (Principle 11)

**Implications:**
- If metaphysical indeterminacy is real, classical logic (bivalence, excluded middle) may need revision for metaphysical claims about the world
- Provides a metaphysical interpretation of quantum superposition that avoids both ignorance and many-worlds
- Connects the metaphysics of vagueness to the philosophy of quantum mechanics in productive new ways

### PRINCIPLE P26 — Powers Metaphysics (Dispositional Essentialism)

**Formal Statement:**
Powers metaphysics (Bird, 2007; Mumford, 2004; Molnar, 2003; Ellis, 2001) holds that at least some fundamental properties are essentially dispositional -- their identity is constituted by what they dispose their bearers to do, not by any categorical (inert, structural) nature. Key claims: (1) Dispositional essentialism (Bird, 2007): natural properties have dispositional essences. Charge essentially disposes its bearer to attract/repel; mass essentially disposes its bearer to resist acceleration. (2) Laws from powers: if properties have dispositional essences, laws of nature are metaphysically necessary -- they flow from the natures of the properties involved, rather than being contingent regularities (contra Humean accounts). This dissolves the inference problem for laws: we know the laws by knowing the natures of properties. (3) The Humean challenge (Lewis): the Humean claims all properties are categorical (qualitative, non-modal) and laws are just the best summary of the Humean mosaic. The powers metaphysician responds that categoricalism cannot explain why properties consistently manifest in regular ways (the "Humean void"). (4) Powers and causation: powers provide a built-in account of causal connection -- causation is the manifestation of a power. (5) The identity regress problem (Lowe): if each power is defined by its manifestation, which involves other powers, the network of powers may be viciously circular (responses: holistic structuralism, graph-theoretic models of power identity).

**Plain Language:**
What is an electric charge, really? One view says it is just a property that happens to be associated with certain laws. The powers view says charge IS the power to attract and repel -- that is what it is essentially, not contingently. If this is right, the laws of physics are not cosmic coincidences or divine decrees but flow inevitably from the nature of the properties that exist. Mass just IS the power to resist acceleration; it could not exist without doing so. This view gives a deep, built-in explanation of why the world behaves regularly, something the rival "Humean" view struggles with.

**Historical Context:**
C.B. Martin (1994) and George Molnar (2003, *Powers*) revived the concept. Stephen Mumford (2004, *Laws in Nature*) defended powers-based laws. Alexander Bird (2007, *Nature's Metaphysics*) developed dispositional essentialism as a comprehensive metaphysical system. Brian Ellis (2001, *Scientific Essentialism*) connected it to scientific realism. The debate between powers metaphysics and Humeanism (Lewis, 1986; Loewer, 2012) is among the most active in contemporary metaphysics.

**Depends On:**
- Laws of nature (Principle 2)
- Dispositions and categorical properties (Principle 19)
- Natural kinds (Principle 3)

**Implications:**
- If correct, laws of nature are metaphysically necessary, not contingent, transforming the metaphysics of modality
- Provides an intrinsic account of causation: causal connection is the manifestation of powers
- Connects metaphysics directly to the philosophy of science: the debate informs what it means to discover a law of nature

### PRINCIPLE P27 — Information-Theoretic Metaphysics

**Formal Statement:**
Information-theoretic metaphysics proposes that information is a (or the) fundamental ontological category. Key positions: (1) It from Bit (Wheeler, 1990): every physical quantity derives its existence from binary yes/no questions; physics is informational at its foundation. (2) Informational structural realism (Floridi, 2008; Sayre, 1976): the world is fundamentally constituted by informational structures -- structures defined by differences, relations, and constraints. Physical objects are patterns of information. (3) Digital ontology (Fredkin, Zuse, Wolfram): the universe is a computational process operating on informational states. (4) The Bekenstein bound and holographic principle: the maximum information content of a region of space is proportional to its surface area, not its volume, suggesting that information is more fundamental than space. (5) Quantum information metaphysics (Bub, 2004; Timpson, 2013): quantum mechanics is best understood as a theory about information rather than about waves, particles, or fields. (6) The pancomputationalist thesis (Piccinini, 2015, contra): not everything is computational/informational -- the concept of information must be constrained to avoid triviality.

**Plain Language:**
What if reality at its most fundamental level is not made of matter or energy but of information? This idea draws support from physics: the holographic principle says the information content of a region of space depends on its surface area (like a hologram), not its volume. Quantum mechanics seems to be fundamentally about what can and cannot be known, copied, or communicated. If information is the basic currency of reality, then physics becomes a branch of information theory, and the material world is a pattern of informational structures. Critics worry that calling everything "information" risks making the concept meaningless.

**Historical Context:**
John Archibald Wheeler (1990) coined "it from bit." Rolf Landauer (1961) showed information has physical implications. Jacob Bekenstein (1973) and Stephen Hawking (1974) connected information to black holes. Luciano Floridi (2008, 2011) developed informational structural realism. The holographic principle (Susskind, 't Hooft, 1990s) and the black hole information paradox have pushed information to the center of fundamental physics. The position remains controversial: critics (Timpson, 2013) argue that "information" is ambiguous between Shannon information and semantic information.

**Depends On:**
- Structural realism (Principle 20)
- Laws of nature (Principle 2)
- Modality and essence (Principle 22)

**Implications:**
- If correct, unifies physics, computer science, and metaphysics under a single informational ontology
- The holographic principle and black hole information paradox provide empirical constraints on metaphysical positions
- Raises the hard question: is information a genuine fundamental category, or is "information" just a useful redescription of physical structure?

### PRINCIPLE P28 — Mathematical Universe Hypothesis (Tegmark)

**Formal Statement:**
The Mathematical Universe Hypothesis (MUH; Tegmark, 2008, 2014) proposes that our physical reality is not merely described by mathematics but is a mathematical structure. Key claims: (1) The External Reality Hypothesis (ERH): there exists an external physical reality completely independent of human observers. (2) The MUH follows from the ERH: if reality exists independently of humans, then its description must be free of human "baggage" (concepts that require human interpretation). The only descriptions completely free of human baggage are mathematical structures -- abstract entities defined entirely by their relations. (3) Therefore, physical reality IS a mathematical structure, and we are self-aware substructures within it. (4) The Level IV multiverse: all mathematical structures that are consistent exist as physical realities. Our universe corresponds to one particular mathematical structure; other structures are equally real. (5) The Computable Universe Hypothesis (CUH; Tegmark's refinement): only computable mathematical structures exist, avoiding the measure problem for non-computable structures. (6) Critiques: the MUH faces the "consciousness problem" (Hut, Alford, and Tegmark, 2006): if reality is purely mathematical, how do mathematical structures give rise to subjective experience? It also faces the "mathematical democracy problem": if all structures exist, why does our observed universe have the specific laws it does?

**Plain Language:**
Most physicists believe mathematics describes reality. Max Tegmark goes much further: he argues that reality IS mathematics. You, your brain, the planet, and the entire universe are not just described by equations -- you ARE a mathematical structure. If this sounds crazy, consider that every property of an electron (charge, spin, mass) is a number, and every relationship between electrons is a mathematical relation. Strip away everything that requires human interpretation, and only mathematics remains. The most radical implication: every self-consistent mathematical structure is its own universe. There is a universe described by every possible set of equations, and we just happen to live in this one.

**Historical Context:**
Pythagoras (6th century BCE) first proposed that reality is fundamentally mathematical. Galileo (1623): "the book of nature is written in mathematics." Eugene Wigner (1960) noted the "unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics in the natural sciences." Max Tegmark (2008, "The Mathematical Universe"; 2014, *Our Mathematical Universe*) developed the MUH systematically. The proposal is related to but distinct from Platonism (Principle 5), structural realism (Principle 20), and information-theoretic metaphysics (Principle P27). It remains highly controversial: critics (Jannes, 2009; Hut et al., 2006) argue it conflates the map with the territory.

**Depends On:**
- Structural realism (Principle 20)
- Possible worlds (Principle 11)
- Information-theoretic metaphysics (Principle P27)

**Implications:**
- If correct, resolves Wigner's puzzle: mathematics is "unreasonably effective" because reality literally is mathematics
- Implies a vast multiverse of mathematical structures, each as real as our own, with profound implications for cosmology and the measure problem
- Challenges the distinction between abstract and concrete: mathematical objects are not pale reflections of reality but ARE reality
- The consciousness problem becomes acute: explaining how subjective experience arises from pure mathematical structure is the central difficulty

### PRINCIPLE P29 — Grounding and Metaphysical Explanation

**Formal Statement:**
Grounding (Fine, 2001, 2012; Schaffer, 2009; Rosen, 2010) is a metaphysical relation of non-causal determination: one fact or entity "grounds" another when it metaphysically explains or constitutes it. Key features: (1) Grounding is irreducible: it is not identical to supervenience, logical entailment, or causal explanation. Mental facts may be grounded in physical facts without being identical to them. (2) Asymmetry and irreflexivity: if A grounds B, then B does not ground A, and nothing grounds itself. Grounding establishes a metaphysical hierarchy from more fundamental to less fundamental. (3) Full vs partial grounding: a conjunctive fact (P and Q) is fully grounded in P and Q together, while each conjunct only partially grounds it. (4) Paradigm cases: (a) The biological grounds the psychological (mental states are grounded in brain states). (b) The microphysical grounds the macrophysical (a table is grounded in its atoms). (c) Truthmakers ground truths (the existence of snow grounds "snow exists"). (5) Unionism vs separatism (Rosen, 2010): unionists hold that grounding is a single unified relation; separatists argue that "grounding" covers heterogeneous metaphysical relations (composition, constitution, realization, set membership) that should be analyzed separately. (6) The grounding-fundamentality link (Schaffer, 2009): grounding defines what is fundamental -- fundamental entities are those that are not grounded in anything else.

**Plain Language:**
Grounding answers the question: what makes what? Not causally (what brings it into existence) but metaphysically (what constitutes it). Your headache is grounded in neural activity -- not caused by it in the way a hammer causes a nail to go in, but constituted by it in a deeper metaphysical sense. A statue is grounded in the bronze it is made of. A set is grounded in its members. Grounding gives metaphysics a structure: at the bottom are the most fundamental things (perhaps quarks and leptons), and everything else is grounded in them, layer by layer. The concept has become central to contemporary metaphysics because it provides a rigorous framework for asking what is really fundamental.

**Historical Context:**
Aristotle's distinction between prior and posterior. The modern grounding debate was initiated by Kit Fine (2001, "The Question of Realism"), Gideon Rosen (2010, "Metaphysical Dependence"), and Jonathan Schaffer (2009, "On What Grounds What"). The concept has become one of the most discussed topics in 21st-century metaphysics, with over 1,000 publications since 2010. Critics (Wilson, 2014; Hofweber, 2009) argue that grounding is obscure, circular, or reduces to other relations.

**Depends On:**
- Composition (mereology) (Principle 8)
- Supervenience and reduction (Principle 14)
- Laws of nature (Principle 2)

**Implications:**
- Provides a unified framework for metaphysical explanation: what makes what, from fundamental physics to moral facts
- Clarifies debates about reductionism: grounded entities are real (not eliminated) but dependent on what grounds them
- Defines fundamentality: the most fundamental entities are those that ground others without being grounded themselves
- Connects to philosophy of mind (is consciousness grounded in physical states?), philosophy of mathematics (are mathematical facts grounded?), and metaethics (are moral facts grounded in natural facts?)

### PRINCIPLE P30 — Digital Ontology and Simulation Metaphysics

**Formal Statement:**
Digital ontology and the simulation hypothesis represent two closely related proposals about the computational nature of reality. (1) Digital ontology (Fredkin, 1990; Zuse, 1969; Wolfram, 2002): the universe is fundamentally a cellular automaton or digital computation. Konrad Zuse proposed the "Rechnender Raum" (computing space) -- that physics is the output of a deterministic computation on a discrete lattice. Edward Fredkin's "digital philosophy" argues that information is more fundamental than matter or energy, and all physical processes are computations on bits. Wolfram (2002, *A New Kind of Science*) proposed that simple computational rules (cellular automata) can generate all the complexity observed in nature. (2) The Simulation Argument (Bostrom, 2003): a trilemma -- at least one of the following is true: (a) almost all civilizations at our level go extinct before becoming technologically mature, (b) almost no technologically mature civilizations run ancestor simulations, or (c) we are almost certainly living in a computer simulation. The argument is not that we ARE simulated but that we must accept at least one of (a), (b), or (c). (3) Philosophical implications: if we are in a simulation, the "laws of physics" are the rules of the simulation, and the simulator plays the role traditionally assigned to God. (4) Empirical testability: Beane, Davoudi, and Savage (2012) proposed that a discrete lattice structure underlying a simulation might produce detectable anisotropies in ultra-high-energy cosmic rays. (5) Critiques: the simulation argument assumes substrate independence of consciousness and that computational resources sufficient for universe-scale simulation are physically possible. Piccinini (2015) argues pancomputationalism trivializes the claim; Floridi (2005) distinguishes informational ontology from simulation metaphysics.

**Plain Language:**
Could the universe be a computer program? Some physicists and philosophers think so. Konrad Zuse proposed that the universe is literally a giant computer running on a discrete grid. Nick Bostrom went further: if advanced civilizations can simulate entire universes (including conscious beings), and if they want to, then the number of simulated beings vastly exceeds real ones -- so we are probably simulated. This is not science fiction; it is a logical argument. If you accept that consciousness can exist on any computational substrate and that civilizations can build powerful enough computers, the math pushes you toward the uncomfortable conclusion that our reality is probably someone else's simulation.

**Historical Context:**
Konrad Zuse (1969, *Rechnender Raum*) first proposed computational physics. Edward Fredkin (1990s) developed digital philosophy. Stephen Wolfram (2002) published *A New Kind of Science*. Nick Bostrom (2003, "Are You Living in a Computer Simulation?") formulated the trilemma, which has become one of the most discussed arguments in 21st-century philosophy. The argument connects to Wheeler's "it from bit" (Principle P27), Tegmark's MUH (Principle P28), and the metaphysics of possible worlds (Principle 11). Elon Musk's public endorsement popularized the idea, though the philosophical argument has far more nuance than popular discussion suggests.

**Depends On:**
- Information-theoretic metaphysics (Principle P27)
- Possible worlds (Principle 11)
- Identity and persistence (Principle 4)

**Implications:**
- If the simulation argument is sound, it constrains our metaphysical beliefs: we must reject at least one seemingly plausible premise
- Digital ontology predicts a discrete spacetime structure that might be empirically detectable at the Planck scale
- Raises profound questions about the nature of physical laws: are they discovered (as in realism) or programmed?
- The ethical implications of creating simulated conscious beings connect to the problem of artificial consciousness

### PRINCIPLE P31 — Computational Metaphysics and Automated Ontology

**Formal Statement:**
Computational metaphysics uses automated reasoning tools (theorem provers, model finders, SAT solvers) to investigate and adjudicate metaphysical questions. Key developments: (1) Oppenheimer and Zalta (2011, "A Computationally-Discovered Simplification of the Ontological Argument"): using the Prover9 automated theorem prover, they formalized a version of the ontological argument within Edward Zalta's axiomatic metaphysics (object theory) and discovered a simpler proof than any human had found. The computer proved that from Zalta's axioms plus one additional premise, the existence of an abstract object that encodes all properties and necessarily exists follows. (2) Formal ontology engineering: projects like SUMO (Suggested Upper Merged Ontology; Niles and Pease, 2001), BFO (Basic Formal Ontology; Smith et al., 2015), and DOLCE (Descriptive Ontology for Linguistic and Cognitive Engineering; Masolo et al., 2003) create rigorous, axiomatized ontological frameworks verified by automated tools. These are used in biomedical informatics, AI knowledge representation, and the Semantic Web. (3) Automated consistency checking: theorem provers can verify that metaphysical axiom systems are consistent, or find models showing they are satisfiable. This transforms metaphysics from a purely discursive activity to one with formal verification. (4) Neural-symbolic metaphysics: emerging work uses large language models and neuro-symbolic systems to explore metaphysical possibility spaces, generate ontological hypotheses, and evaluate their formal properties. (5) Limitations: formalization inevitably simplifies; the move from natural-language metaphysics to formal axioms may lose essential content (the "formalization gap").

**Plain Language:**
What if we could use computers to settle philosophical arguments? Computational metaphysics tries to do exactly that. Philosophers have been debating questions about existence, necessity, and categories for millennia. Now, automated theorem provers can take formalized versions of these arguments and check whether they are valid. In one striking case, a computer found a simpler proof of a version of the ontological argument for God's existence than any human philosopher had discovered. Meanwhile, ontology engineers build formal, machine-readable systems of categories (what kinds of things exist, how they relate) used in everything from medical databases to AI reasoning. The key question is: when a computer settles a metaphysical dispute, has it really resolved the philosophical issue, or has it merely analyzed a simplified formal model?

**Historical Context:**
Leibniz (17th century) dreamed of a "calculus ratiocinator" that would resolve philosophical disputes through computation. Edward Zalta's axiomatic theory of abstract objects (1983, 1988) provided a formal framework rich enough for computational metaphysics. Oppenheimer and Zalta (2011) demonstrated automated metaphysical reasoning. The SUMO ontology (Niles and Pease, 2001) and BFO (Barry Smith et al.) represent large-scale formal ontology projects. The field connects to the broader movement of experimental philosophy (X-phi) and the computational humanities, representing a transformation of metaphysics from armchair reflection to computationally-aided formal investigation.

**Depends On:**
- Abstract objects (Principle 21)
- Ontological categories (Principle 7)
- Grounding and metaphysical explanation (Principle P29)

**Implications:**
- Transforms metaphysics into a partially computational discipline where formal consistency and consequence can be machine-verified
- Reveals hidden assumptions and simplifications in classical philosophical arguments when they are rigorously formalized
- Formal ontologies have practical applications in AI, medicine, and data integration, making metaphysics directly relevant to technology
- Raises the meta-question: does formalizing a metaphysical argument preserve its philosophical content, or does essential meaning evaporate in translation?

### PRINCIPLE P32 — Ontic Structural Realism

**Formal Statement:**
Ontic structural realism (OSR, Ladyman 1998, French and Ladyman 2003) holds that the fundamental ontology of the world consists of structures (relations, symmetries) rather than individual objects. Two variants: (1) Eliminative OSR (French, Ladyman): there are no individual objects at the fundamental level — only structure. Quantum particles are not individuals but nodes in a relational structure, as evidenced by permutation invariance and entanglement. (2) Moderate OSR (Esfeld, Lam): objects exist but are constituted by their relational properties — there is nothing to objects beyond their place in a structure. Motivation from physics: (a) Underdetermination of individuality: quantum mechanics is equally compatible with particles being individuals (with haecceity) or non-individuals; OSR dissolves this underdetermination by eliminating individuals. (b) Theory change: Worrall's (1989) structural realism argued that mathematical structure survives theory change (Fresnel to Maxwell), but objects do not. OSR radicalizes this: structure is all there is. (c) Gauge symmetries: the fundamental entities of modern physics (gauge fields, fiber bundles) are inherently structural/relational.

**Plain Language:**
What is the world fundamentally made of? Ontic structural realism gives a radical answer: not things, but relations and patterns. At the quantum level, particles are not tiny billiard balls with individual identities — they are interchangeable nodes in a web of relations. What survives when physics undergoes a revolution is the mathematical structure, not the things the theory posits. So maybe the structure is what is real, and the "things" are just convenient ways of talking about patterns in the structure.

**Historical Context:**
Worrall (1989, epistemic structural realism). Ladyman (1998, ontic structural realism). French and Ladyman (2003, "Remodelling Structural Realism"). Esfeld (2004, moderate OSR). French (2014, *The Structure of the World*). Critiques from Psillos (2006, the "nature of structure" objection) and Chakravartty (2004, the need for causal properties).

**Depends On:**
- Scientific Realism (Principle 6)
- Spacetime (Principle 2)
- Symmetry and Invariance (Principle 4)

**Implications:**
- Provides the most naturalistic metaphysics aligned with modern physics
- Dissolves metaphysical puzzles about particle individuality in quantum mechanics
- The "no miracles" argument for realism is preserved for structural content
- Challenges object-oriented metaphysics that dominates everyday and philosophical thinking

### PRINCIPLE P33 — Metametaphysics and Ontological Deflation

**Formal Statement:**
Metametaphysics investigates the methodology, epistemology, and legitimacy of metaphysical inquiry itself. Key positions: (1) Ontological realism (Sider 2011, *Writing the Book of the World*): there is a privileged, "joint-carving" ontological structure to reality; metaphysical questions have objective answers. Quantifier variance is false — "exists" has one correct meaning. (2) Ontological anti-realism / deflationism (Carnap 1950, Thomasson 2015): metaphysical disputes (e.g., "do numbers exist?") are either verbal disputes about how to use words, or they are to be settled by pragmatic criteria rather than by discovering deep facts. Carnap distinguished "internal" questions (answered within a framework) from "external" questions (about which framework to adopt), arguing external questions are pragmatic, not factual. (3) Quantifier variance (Hirsch 2011): different quantifier meanings can be equally good; metaphysical disputes between, say, mereological universalists and nihilists are merely verbal. (4) Grounding-based metametaphysics: metaphysical structure is revealed by grounding relations (Fine 2012); the world has a layered structure with fundamental and derivative levels. Metaphysics investigates this layered structure.

**Plain Language:**
Is metaphysics real or just playing with words? When philosophers debate whether numbers exist or whether wholes are more than their parts, are they discovering deep truths or just arguing about language? Metametaphysics tackles this question. Some philosophers insist reality has a definite structure that metaphysics can discover. Others argue that many metaphysical disputes are merely verbal — different equally valid ways of describing the same world. The debate about the legitimacy of metaphysics is itself one of the most active areas of contemporary philosophy.

**Historical Context:**
Carnap (1950, "Empiricism, Semantics, and Ontology"). Quine (1948, "On What There Is"). Chalmers, Manley, Wasserman (2009, *Metametaphysics*). Sider (2011, *Writing the Book of the World*). Hirsch (2011, *Quantifier Variance and Realism*). Thomasson (2015, *Ontology Made Easy*). Fine (2012, "Guide to Ground").

**Depends On:**
- Ontological Categories (Principle 7)
- Grounding (Principle P29)
- Abstract Objects (Principle 21)

**Implications:**
- Determines whether metaphysical inquiry is a legitimate route to knowledge or merely conceptual analysis
- The grounding revolution has revitalized metaphysics by providing a rigorous framework for layered ontology
- If quantifier variance is correct, many traditional metaphysical disputes dissolve
- Implications for AI ontology: is there a "correct" ontology for AI to learn, or are ontologies pragmatic tools?

### PRINCIPLE P34 — Mereology and the Composition Question

**Formal Statement:**
Mereology is the formal theory of parts and wholes. Classical extensional mereology (Lesniewski 1916, Goodman and Leonard 1940) axiomatizes the part-of relation as reflexive, antisymmetric, and transitive, with supplementation and (optionally) unrestricted composition. The Special Composition Question (van Inwagen 1990): under what conditions do some objects compose a further object? Three main positions: (1) Universalism (Lewis 1986): any objects whatsoever compose something — there exists a fusion of your left shoe and the Eiffel Tower. (2) Nihilism (Dorr 2005, Sider 2013): strictly speaking, there are no composite objects — only fundamental simples arranged in various ways ("there are no tables, only particles arranged table-wise"). (3) Restricted composition (van Inwagen 1990): composition occurs only when particles participate in a life — biological organisms are the only genuine composite objects. The General Composition Question (Schaffer 2009): given that wholes exist, what is the relation between a whole and its parts? Is it identity (composition as identity, Baxter 1988)? Is it grounding (the whole is grounded in its parts)? Or is it the reverse (priority monism)?

**Plain Language:**
When you have some objects, do they automatically form a bigger object? Is a heap of sand one thing or many? Is there really a "thing" that is the sum of your nose and the moon? Mereology tackles these surprisingly deep questions. Some philosophers say any collection of things forms a whole (universalism). Others say there are no composite objects at all — just fundamental particles arranged in patterns (nihilism). The answers matter: if universalism is true, there are vastly more objects than common sense suggests; if nihilism is true, tables and chairs are useful fictions.

**Historical Context:**
Lesniewski (1916) developed formal mereology. Goodman and Leonard (1940) introduced calculus of individuals. Lewis (1991, *Parts of Classes*) defended universalism. Van Inwagen (1990, *Material Beings*) posed the Special Composition Question. Sider (2001, *Four-Dimensionalism*) connected mereology to temporal parts. Schaffer (2009, 2010) proposed priority monism as an alternative.

**Depends On:**
- Ontological Categories (Principle 7)
- Grounding (Principle P29)
- Abstract Objects (Principle 21)

**Implications:**
- Determines the number and kind of objects that exist — a fundamental ontological question
- Connects to physics: are composite objects (atoms, molecules) ontologically fundamental or derivative?
- The nihilism-universalism spectrum maps onto practical questions about the ontology of social entities
- Informs AI ontology: what objects should knowledge representations include?

### PRINCIPLE P35 — Priority Monism and Metaphysical Holism

**Formal Statement:**
Priority monism (Schaffer 2010) holds that the cosmos (the one maximal concrete object) is the fundamental entity, and all proper parts are derivative — existing in virtue of the whole rather than the reverse. This inverts the standard "building-up" picture where fundamental physics identifies the smallest particles as basic and everything else is derivative. Arguments: (1) The argument from quantum entanglement: in quantum mechanics, the state of a composite system is not determined by the states of its parts (non-separability); the whole is prior to its parts. (2) The argument from laws: fundamental laws govern the state of the whole universe (the universal wave function), not individual particles. (3) Historical precedent: monism dominated metaphysics from Parmenides through Spinoza, Bradley, and the British Idealists. The position differs from existence monism (only one thing exists) — Schaffer's monism is about priority (grounding), not existence. Everything exists; the cosmos is just more fundamental than its parts.

**Plain Language:**
Commonsense says reality is built from the bottom up: tiny particles combine into atoms, atoms into molecules, molecules into cells, and so on. Priority monism turns this upside down: the whole universe is the fundamental thing, and its parts are derivative. This is not as strange as it sounds — quantum mechanics tells us that entangled particles do not have independent states; only the whole system has a definite state. If fundamental physics describes the universe as a whole, maybe the universe really is more fundamental than its parts.

**Historical Context:**
Parmenides and Spinoza defended forms of monism. Bradley (1893, *Appearance and Reality*) argued for idealist monism. The analytic tradition rejected monism in favor of pluralism (Russell, Moore). Schaffer (2010, "Monism: The Priority of the Whole") revived monism using modern tools. Ismael and Schaffer (2020) argued from quantum entanglement. The position connects to quantum holism (Healey 1991, Teller 1986) and structural realism.

**Depends On:**
- Grounding (Principle P29)
- Mereology (Principle P34)
- Ontic Structural Realism (Principle P32)

**Implications:**
- If correct, the building-up picture of fundamental physics is wrong — the cosmos is more fundamental than quarks
- Quantum entanglement provides empirical motivation: entangled systems are genuinely holistic
- Challenges reductionism: wholes may be explanatorily prior to their parts
- Connects analytic metaphysics to the holistic traditions of process philosophy and Eastern thought

### PRINCIPLE P36 — Process Metaphysics and Process Ontology

**Formal Statement:**
Process metaphysics (Whitehead 1929; Rescher 1996; Seibt 2003) holds that the fundamental constituents of reality are processes (events, activities, becomings) rather than substances (things, objects, beings). Whitehead's process philosophy: the ultimate real things are "actual occasions of experience" — momentary events of self-creative becoming. Each actual occasion "prehends" (takes account of) prior occasions and contributes data to future ones. There are no enduring substances; what we call "objects" are recurrent patterns of process (nexus of actual occasions). Seibt's (2003) General Process Theory (GPT) provides a formal ontology: processes are classified by their dynamicity profile (activities, developments, events) and by their participant structure (subjectless processes like "it rains" are primitive, not derived from substance-attribute combinations). Dupre (2012, *Processes of Life*) argues that biology demands process ontology: organisms are not things but self-maintaining processes — a cell is a process that maintains its own conditions of existence. Formal framework: a process p is characterized by its temporal profile tau(p), its participant structure pi(p), and its modal profile mu(p) (how it could develop differently).

**Plain Language:**
Western metaphysics since Aristotle has assumed reality is fundamentally made of things — substances that persist through time and have changing properties. Process metaphysics flips this: reality is fundamentally made of happenings, becomings, activities. A river is not a thing but a process of flowing. A person is not a substance but a process of living. Even an atom is a pattern of activity, not a static object. This is not just philosophical wordplay: biology makes far more sense in process terms (organisms are self-maintaining processes, not machines), and modern physics (where "particles" are excitations of quantum fields) aligns better with process than substance ontology.

**Historical Context:**
Heraclitus (~500 BCE) held that reality is flux. Whitehead (1929, *Process and Reality*) developed the definitive process metaphysics. Rescher (1996, *Process Metaphysics*) modernized the tradition. Seibt (2003) formalized process ontology for analytic metaphysics. Dupre (2012) and Nicholson and Dupre (2018, *Everything Flows*) applied process thinking to biology. Ladyman and Ross (2007, *Every Thing Must Go*) developed structural process realism. The tradition is experiencing a revival due to its natural fit with quantum field theory and biological self-organization.

**Depends On:**
- Causation (Principle 1)
- Identity and Persistence (Principle 4)
- Temporal Parts vs Endurantism (Principle 17)

**Implications:**
- Offers an alternative to substance metaphysics that aligns better with modern physics and biology
- Process ontology dissolves the persistence problem: there is nothing that needs to persist, only patterns that recur
- Provides philosophical foundations for understanding biological organisms, ecosystems, and social institutions as processes
- Connects to indigenous ontologies (many non-Western philosophical traditions are process-based)

### PRINCIPLE P37 — Social Ontology and the Metaphysics of Institutions

**Formal Statement:**
Social ontology (Searle 1995, 2010; Tuomela 2013; Epstein 2015) investigates the metaphysical nature of social entities — institutions, money, borders, corporations, laws. Searle's constitutive rule framework: social facts are created by collective acceptance of constitutive rules of the form "X counts as Y in context C" (e.g., "this piece of paper counts as money in the US"). The key mechanism: collective intentionality — the capacity for shared intentional states ("we believe that...," "we accept that..."). Deontic powers (rights, duties, permissions) are created by status functions: money has the status function of a medium of exchange, conferring on its holders the deontic power to make purchases. Epstein (2015, *The Ant Trap*) argues Searle's framework is too narrow: some social facts are "anchored" by social structures rather than individual mental states — the institution of racism exists not because anyone collectively intends it but because social structures produce it. Guala (2016) proposes a rules-in-equilibrium account: institutions are rules that persist because following them is a Nash equilibrium.

**Plain Language:**
What kind of thing is money? A border? A corporation? These are not physical objects (a dollar bill's physical properties do not explain its purchasing power) and not purely mental (they exist whether any individual thinks about them). Social ontology investigates the metaphysical nature of these peculiar entities. Searle's answer: social reality is created by collective agreement — we all accept that certain pieces of paper are money, and that acceptance makes it so. But critics point out that some social realities (like structural racism) exist without anyone's explicit agreement. The deepest question: can social institutions be reduced to individual mental states, or do they have an irreducible social reality?

**Historical Context:**
Searle (1995, *The Construction of Social Reality*) established the modern field. Tuomela (2002, 2013) developed the collective acceptance account. Bratman (1999, 2014) analyzed shared cooperative activity. Epstein (2015) challenged individualistic social ontology. Guala (2016, *Understanding Institutions*) proposed the game-theoretic account. Gilbert (2014) developed the "joint commitment" framework. The field connects to legal philosophy (what makes a contract valid?), economics (what are money and markets?), and AI (can AI systems participate in institutional reality?).

**Depends On:**
- Ontological Categories (Principle 7)
- Grounding and Metaphysical Explanation (Principle P29)
- Supervenience (Principle 13)

**Implications:**
- Explains the ontological status of entities (money, laws, corporations) that are ubiquitous but philosophically puzzling
- Connects to AI: can AI systems create or participate in institutional facts? (If a contract is signed by an AI agent, does it have deontic force?)
- The debate between individualist and structuralist social ontology has practical implications for addressing systemic problems like racism
- Provides philosophical foundations for understanding digital institutions (DAOs, smart contracts, virtual currencies)

### PRINCIPLE P38 — Grounding and Metaphysical Explanation (Fine/Rosen/Schaffer)

**Formal Statement:**
Grounding (Fine 2001, 2012; Rosen 2010; Schaffer 2009) is a relation of non-causal, metaphysical explanation: the fact that [P] grounds the fact that [Q] when Q obtains in virtue of P. Notation: [P] < [Q] (P grounds Q, or Q holds because of P). Key properties: (1) irreflexivity — no fact grounds itself, (2) asymmetry — if [P] < [Q], then not [Q] < [P], (3) transitivity — if [P] < [Q] and [Q] < [R], then [P] < [R], (4) non-monotonicity — adding grounds can block grounding relations, (5) necessitation — if [P] < [Q], then necessarily, if [P] then [Q] (debated). Examples: the mental is grounded in the physical (physicalism), the normative is grounded in the natural (naturalism), the dispositional is grounded in the categorical (categoricalism), sets are grounded in their members. Fine's distinction: grounding is not identity, not supervenience, not entailment — it is a sui generis explanatory relation. Schaffer's (2009) priority monism: grounding structures reality into a hierarchy from fundamental to derived, with the cosmos (the whole) being the one fundamental entity from which parts are derived. The debate: is grounding a unified, primitive relation, or is it a family of heterogeneous determination relations (Wilson 2014)?

**Plain Language:**
When we say "the painting is beautiful because of the arrangement of colors," the "because" is not causal — it is explanatory in a deeper, metaphysical sense. Grounding captures this: one fact holds in virtue of another. The mind exists in virtue of the brain. A law exists in virtue of social practices. A set exists in virtue of its members. Grounding has become the central structuring concept in contemporary metaphysics because it organizes reality into levels — from fundamental physics up through chemistry, biology, psychology, and sociology — where each level is grounded in the level below. The deepest question: is grounding a single, unified relation, or just a label for many different ways things can depend on other things?

**Historical Context:**
Aristotle's "four causes" included formal and final causes as non-causal explanations. Fine (2001, "The Question of Realism") revived grounding. Rosen (2010, "Metaphysical Dependence") provided the formal framework. Schaffer (2009, "On What Grounds What") argued grounding is the central question of metaphysics. Audi (2012) and Dasgupta (2014) developed the theory further. Wilson (2014, *No Work for a Theory of Grounding*) argued grounding should be eliminated in favor of specific determination relations. The debate has restructured metaphysics: questions about reduction, emergence, and the layered structure of reality are now framed in grounding terms.

**Depends On:**
- Ontological Categories (Principle 7)
- Supervenience (Principle 13)
- Modality and Possible Worlds (Principle 11)

**Implications:**
- Provides the conceptual framework for structuring reality into fundamental and derived levels
- Replaces the ambiguous notion of "reduction" with a more precise metaphysical relation
- The grounding framework allows stating physicalism, naturalism, and other "ism"s with unprecedented precision
- Connects to the philosophy of science: scientific explanation often involves non-causal, grounding-like relations (mathematical explanation, constitutive explanation)

### PRINCIPLE P39 — Dispositionalism and Powers Metaphysics (Mumford/Bird/Vetter)

**Formal Statement:**
Dispositionalism (Mumford 1998; Bird 2007; Vetter 2015) holds that properties are essentially dispositional — defined by what they dispose their bearers to do — rather than categorical (brute, qualitative). A property F is dispositional if possessing F essentially involves a disposition to produce manifestation M in stimulus conditions S: F(x) -> (Sx -> Mx). Bird's (2007) strong dispositionalism: all fundamental properties are powers (dispositions), and the laws of nature are metaphysically necessary consequences of the natures of these properties — there are no contingent laws. The "always packing, never traveling" objection (Armstrong): if all properties are dispositions, then everything is defined in terms of what it would do, never what it is — an infinite regress of potentiality with no categorical ground. Vetter's (2015) potentiality metaphysics resolves this: dispositions are not conditional (if S then M) but unconditional potentialities — an object can simply have the potentiality to manifest M, full stop, without specifying stimulus conditions. Mumford and Anjum (2011) argue that powers dispose rather than necessitate: causation is the manifestation of powers, and powers produce tendencies, not certainties — explaining both causal regularity and the possibility of prevention/interference.

**Plain Language:**
What is a property, fundamentally? Is the charge of an electron just a brute fact about what the electron is (categorical), or is it defined by what the electron does — attract, repel, create fields (dispositional)? Dispositionalism says properties are defined by their powers: to be fragile just is to be disposed to break when struck. The radical version says all of reality is made of powers — there are no brute, inert properties at the bottom. This has a remarkable consequence: the laws of nature are not contingent regularities imposed from outside but necessary consequences of the natures of the properties themselves. Nature could not have had different laws because the laws flow from what properties essentially are.

**Historical Context:**
Aristotle's potentiality/actuality distinction is the origin. Ryle (1949) analyzed dispositions linguistically. Prior, Pargetter, and Jackson (1982) defined dispositions via conditional analysis. Martin (1994) and Mumford (1998) developed the powers ontology. Bird (2007, *Nature's Metaphysics*) argued all properties are powers. Vetter (2015, *Potentiality*) developed the non-conditional account. Mumford and Anjum (2011, *Getting Causes from Powers*) connected powers to causation. The view has gained substantial support as an alternative to the Humean metaphysics of Lewis, offering a modally rich ontology where properties necessitate their nomic roles.

**Depends On:**
- Laws of Nature (Principle 3)
- Causation (Principle 5)
- Modality and Possible Worlds (Principle 11)

**Implications:**
- If dispositionalism is correct, laws of nature are metaphysically necessary — there are no possible worlds with different laws
- Provides an anti-Humean metaphysics where nature has genuine causal powers, not just regularities
- Resolves the problem of induction: if properties necessitate their effects, the future must resemble the past (given the same properties)
- Connects to philosophy of science: scientific laws describe the essential natures of properties, not mere observed patterns
