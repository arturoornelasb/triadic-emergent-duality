# 07 — Formal Sciences

> *"The laws of logic are the laws of thought."* — George Boole

## Scope

The formal sciences study abstract formal systems — structures of reasoning that are defined by rules rather than empirical observation. Unlike empirical sciences, formal sciences are concerned with **validity** rather than **truth about the physical world**. They provide the scaffolding upon which both mathematical and empirical sciences are built.

## Branches Covered

| Branch | Directory | Focus |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| **Mathematical Logic** | `mathematical_logic/` | Propositional logic, predicate logic, model theory, proof theory |
| **Set Theory** | `set_theory/` | ZFC axioms, ordinals, cardinals, axiom of choice |
| **Category Theory** | `category_theory/` | Categories, functors, natural transformations, universal properties |
| **Decision Theory** | `decision_theory/` | Utility axioms, expected value, rational choice |
| **Game Theory** | `game_theory/` | Nash equilibrium, dominant strategies, mechanism design |
| **Systems Theory** | `systems_theory/` | Feedback, emergence, self-organization, cybernetics |

## Research Approach

1. Start with **Mathematical Logic** — the most basic formal system
2. **Set Theory** — the standard foundation of mathematics
3. **Category Theory** — an alternative foundational perspective
4. **Decision Theory** and **Game Theory** — formal models of rational choice
5. **Systems Theory** — formal principles of complex systems
6. For each: identify the **axiom system** and its key consequences

## Key Questions

- What is the relationship between logic and mathematics?
- Can all of mathematics be founded on set theory alone?
- What does category theory offer as an alternative foundation?
- Are the axioms of rational choice (decision theory) truly first principles or cultural assumptions?
